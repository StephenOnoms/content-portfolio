5 Serverless Inference Platforms Worth Watching in 2026

~16 min read · Writing sample

The serverless inference market has moved fast enough that a provider list from even a year ago is already out of date. If you're choosing where to run open-weight models in 2026, the short answer is: there's no single "best" platform — the right pick depends on whether you care most about model breadth, raw price, developer control, or zero cold starts, because the providers below have deliberately specialized rather than converged on one model.

Here's a practical look at five platforms worth evaluating, what each is actually optimized for, where each one falls short, and how to think about choosing between them — plus a few more worth knowing about even if they didn't make the main five.

Key Takeaways

  • Featherless stands out for sheer model catalog breadth and flat-rate pricing — strong for teams that want to experiment across many open-weight models without re-budgeting per model.
  • Together AI offers an OpenAI-compatible API and per-token pricing that's competitive at moderate volumes, plus dedicated and fine-tuning options as you scale.
  • Replicate prioritizes zero-deployment-code access to popular models, with predictable output-based pricing on its "Official Models."
  • Hugging Face Inference Endpoints is the natural choice if your workflow already lives in the HF ecosystem and you want production-grade, instance-based hosting.
  • Modal suits teams that want full custom code control and are comfortable with per-second GPU billing in exchange for flexibility.
  • Pricing on the same model can spread several-fold across providers — always benchmark your actual workload, not headline rates.

What "Serverless Inference" Actually Means Here

Every platform in this roundup removes the need to provision and manage your own GPU servers — you call an API (or deploy a function), and the platform handles loading the model, allocating GPU capacity, and scaling. Where they differ is how much of the model catalog is pre-built for you, how pricing is structured, and how much custom code you can run.

How We Evaluated These Platforms

Rather than rank by a single "best overall" score — which tends to hide the trade-offs that actually matter — each platform below is assessed against four criteria:

  • Model catalog breadth: how many open-weight models are available out of the box, and how quickly new releases get added.
  • Pricing predictability: how easy it is to forecast a monthly bill before you've sent a single request.
  • Developer control: how much you can customize the serving environment versus working within a fixed API.
  • Ecosystem fit: how well the platform integrates with tools and workflows teams are already using (Hugging Face, LangChain, OpenAI-compatible SDKs, etc).

No platform scores highly on all four — and that's the point. The "best" platform is the one whose strengths line up with what your team actually needs most.

The Five Platforms

1. Featherless

Featherless positions itself around breadth: a very large catalog of open-weight models (in the thousands), available through a single OpenAI-compatible API, priced on a flat-rate/concurrency basis rather than per token. For teams that want to try multiple open-weight models — or switch between them — without negotiating a new price per model, this removes a lot of friction.

  • Pros: enormous model selection, predictable monthly cost regardless of which models you call, OpenAI-compatible API simplifies migration.
  • Cons: flat-rate pricing means very low-traffic projects may pay for headroom they don't use; less suited to teams wanting per-second billing transparency.
  • Good fit if: you're building something that needs to evaluate or serve many different open models, or you want budget predictability from day one.
  • Probably not for you if: you have extremely low, sporadic traffic and want to pay literally nothing between requests.

2. Together AI

Together AI offers an OpenAI-compatible serverless API with per-token pricing that varies by model, plus dedicated endpoints (hourly GPU rental) and fine-tuning pipelines for teams that outgrow shared infrastructure.

  • Pros: OpenAI-compatible API eases migration; clear upgrade path from serverless to dedicated as traffic grows; fine-tuning support under the same account.
  • Cons: per-token rates vary by model, so "switching models" can mean re-pricing your workload; dedicated endpoints require capacity planning.
  • Good fit if: you've settled on one or two models and want a clear path from prototype to dedicated capacity without changing providers.
  • Probably not for you if: you need a single flat price across a very wide range of models.

3. Replicate

Replicate's strength is removing deployment work entirely for popular models — its "Official Models" program offers fixed, output-based pricing for well-known models with no cold-start billing surprises.

  • Pros: fastest path from "I found a model" to "I'm calling it in production"; predictable per-output pricing on Official Models removes a whole class of billing surprises.
  • Cons: convenience carries a price premium versus raw GPU-hour costs elsewhere; community models still bill per-second, including cold starts.
  • Good fit if: you're prototyping, shipping a smaller-scale production feature, or want to avoid writing any deployment code at all.
  • Probably not for you if: you're running high-volume traffic where the per-call convenience premium adds up significantly over raw infrastructure cost.

4. Hugging Face Inference Endpoints

For teams already working from the Hugging Face Hub, Inference Endpoints offers a more traditional instance-based hosting model — you choose hardware, and HF manages the deployment of (in principle) any model on the Hub.

  • Pros: works with effectively any model on the Hub, not just a curated catalog; familiar to teams already using HF tooling for training/fine-tuning.
  • Cons: instance-based billing means you're paying for the instance whether or not it's fully utilized, unless scale-to-zero is configured and cold starts are acceptable.
  • Good fit if: your model is already on the Hub, your team is HF-native, and you want production hosting without leaving that ecosystem.
  • Probably not for you if: you want usage-based pricing with no instance-management decisions at all.

5. Modal

Modal is the choice for teams that want to write and deploy their own Python-based serving code rather than rely on a pre-built model catalog, billed by the second of GPU time used.

  • Pros: maximum flexibility — custom batching, multi-step pipelines, any model format; pay only for active GPU seconds.
  • Cons: you own the serving code, including cold-start mitigation (e.g. caching weights on persistent volumes); steepest learning curve of the five.
  • Good fit if: your use case genuinely needs custom serving logic that a pre-built API can't express.
  • Probably not for you if: you'd rather not write or maintain serving infrastructure at all.

Pro tip

Pricing for the same underlying model can vary several-fold across these platforms, and the cheapest option shifts as new models and providers launch. Treat any pricing comparison — including this one — as a starting point for your own benchmark, not a final answer. Re-check before committing to a provider for a production workload.

Honorable Mentions

The market is bigger than five providers, and a few others are worth knowing about even though they didn't make the main comparison:

  • Fireworks AI — another OpenAI-compatible, per-token platform with a focus on fast inference for popular open models; worth comparing against Together AI for the same workloads.
  • Groq — runs models on custom LPU hardware rather than GPUs, with a focus on very low-latency token generation; a strong option when generation speed itself is the priority, though model selection is narrower.
  • Baseten — positions itself between Modal's full code control and Replicate's pre-built convenience, with a model deployment workflow aimed at production ML teams.
  • RunPod — primarily a budget-focused serverless GPU provider; relevant if raw GPU-hour cost is the dominant factor and you're comfortable with a more infrastructure-first workflow.

How to Choose

In short, the decision usually comes down to one dominant constraint:

  • If model variety matters most (you're experimenting, building a multi-model product, or don't want to re-negotiate pricing per model) — breadth-first, flat-rate platforms like Featherless reduce friction.
  • If you're optimizing for a small number of well-known models at scale — per-token platforms like Together AI often win on cost predictability as volume grows.
  • If engineering time is your scarcest resource — zero-deploy platforms like Replicate trade a price premium for speed to production.
  • If you're already deep in the Hugging Face ecosystem — Inference Endpoints keeps your workflow in one place.
  • If you need full control over serving logic (custom batching, unusual model formats, multi-step pipelines) — Modal's code-first model is hard to beat, provided you can absorb the ops overhead.

FAQ

Can I use more than one of these platforms at once?

Yes, and many production teams do — for example, using a breadth-first platform for experimentation and a per-token or dedicated platform for the one or two models that make it to production. The OpenAI-compatible API pattern shared by several of these platforms makes it relatively straightforward to route different workloads to different providers.

Is the cheapest provider always the best choice?

Not necessarily. Headline price differences are often smaller than the cost of engineering time spent integrating, monitoring, and maintaining a more complex setup. For early-stage products especially, the platform that gets you to production fastest can be the better economic choice even at a higher per-token rate.

How often should I re-evaluate this comparison?

Roughly quarterly. New models, pricing changes, and new entrants happen often enough in this market that a comparison done a year ago may no longer reflect the best option for your workload — even if your workload itself hasn't changed.

Final Thoughts

None of these platforms are "winning" in an absolute sense — they're specializing. The practical move for most teams isn't picking one provider forever, but matching workload to provider: a breadth-first platform for experimentation, a per-token or dedicated option once a model proves out, and possibly a code-first platform for anything genuinely custom. Build with that flexibility in mind from day one, and revisit the comparison every couple of quarters — this market moves fast enough that it's worth it.
